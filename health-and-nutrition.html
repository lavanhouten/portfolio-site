Health and Nutrition Analysis¶

The dataset that I chose to investigate was tbe National Health and Nutrition Health Survey 2013-2014 (NHANES) Age Prediction Subset dataset. It contained the age group of individuals and a variety of diabetes-related health data points about them. From those data points, my goal was to predict whether or not an individual is a Senior or an Adult. Along the way, I also aim to investigate which health data points are the most important when determining whether or not someone is a Senior.

This analysis will help us answer two questions:

What health metrics are most indicative of being in the Senior age group?

Can age group be predicted using simple health metrics?

Random Forest¶

The first way I decided to approach the data was with was a Random Forest, as it is well suited for dealing with complex relationships within the data due to it being an ensemble model, and it also has the ability to work well with an imbalanced dataset when balanced class weighting is applied. From here I decided to do a Randomized Search to find the optimal parameters in a reasonable amount of time. I optimized my Randomized Search for the F1 Score in order to value the correctness of both True Positives and True Negatives equally as the dataset is imbalanced. I chose F1 score over Precision or Recall because I don't value predicting if one person is a Senior more than I value predicting a person is an Adult despite the dataset being imbalanced towards the Adult class.

I used all of these parameters in my random search because I wanted to create a wide variety of forest from which the best could be chosen. Specifically I tuned the ranges of 'n_estimators,' 'max_depth,' and 'min_samples_split' in the parameters to discourage any potential forests from overfitting.

Also, I scored the models during cross validation in both F1 Score and Accuracy to keep track of their performance across the different folds in order to make sure I wasn't sacrificing too much Accuracy for the F1 Score increases.

As an aside, I first did testing while searching for the highest accuracy model, but the results had many False Negatives similar to the MLP PCA model down below, making them not particularly useful.

Multilayer Perceptron¶

Next, as a point of comparison, I decided to use Multilayer Perceptron models to predict the labels of the dataset. Based on my previous experience, I knew that they were good at determining complex relationships, and I thought that might be helpful when solving this problem due to the complex relationships between health metrics and age. First, I needed to scale the data because MLPs perform best if the data is normalized, so I applied the MinMaxScaler to the data. I also removed the variables found by the Random Forest to not have any predictive power in order to reduce the noise for the model and improve performance.

Final Results¶

Findings:

  • It appears that the Random Forest is a better model for this data than the Multilayer Perceptron due to its superior ability to deal with the imbalanced training dataset due to it having the highest AUROC (0.74) and F1 Score (0.397)
  • From this data, Gender and Diabetes Diagnosis appear to have no predictive connection to whether an individual is a Senior or not, whereas Blood Insulin Levels, Work Activity, Oral Glucose Tolerance Test Results, Blood Glucose after fasting, and Body Mass Index can be used predict if an individual is in the Adult or Senior age group.
  • Further improvements could definitely be made by adding more features through feature engineering or adding more features to the dataset.
  • Other algorithms that deal with imbalanced data well, such as other tree-based alogrithms like Gradient Boosted Decision Trees, could also provide better performance.
